NASHVILLE SONGWRITING

The "Nashville Number System" is still the fastest way to communicate chord changes in a writing session. If you're co-writing in this town — and you should be — every writer in the room expects you to know it.

Developed in the late 1950s by Neal Matthews Jr. of the Jordanaires, the Nashville Number System replaces traditional chord names with numbers based on the scale degree. A I-IV-V progression in the key of G becomes simply 1-4-5. Change the key? The numbers stay the same.

Here's why it matters: Nashville co-writes move fast. A typical session is 3-4 hours, and the goal is a finished song. Nobody wants to stop and re-chart when the singer says "let's try it in E-flat instead." With the number system, you just play.

The basics you need to know:

  1. Numbers = scale degrees. In any major key, 1 is the root, 4 is the subdominant, 5 is the dominant. A dash (-) after a number means minor. So 6- is the relative minor chord.
  2. Rhythm is shown with underlines and carets. Each number gets one bar unless marked otherwise. A diamond (♦) means hold/sustain. An arrow means push (play the chord an eighth note early).
  3. Slash chords use a slash. 1/3 means the 1 chord with the 3rd in the bass. Simple.
  4. The chart fits on one page. Verse, chorus, bridge — the whole song structure is visible at a glance. No page turns mid-song.

Most Nashville writers chart songs in real-time during the writing session. The ability to read and write number charts fluently is as fundamental here as knowing how to tune your guitar.

If you're new to it, start by converting 5 songs you already know into number charts. Within a week, you'll be thinking in numbers instead of letters — and your next co-write will run twice as smoothly.

What's your favorite Nashville neighborhood for live music?

MUSIC ROW INSIDER

If you're a songwriter looking for a publishing deal in Nashville, 2026 might be your year. Three publishers on Music Row are actively expanding their rosters — and they're specifically looking for fresh voices.

1. Anthem Entertainment (formerly Ole)

Anthem has been on an aggressive signing spree since relocating their Nashville office to a larger space on 17th Avenue South. They're looking for: singer-songwriters who can write across genres (country, pop-country, Americana), and producers who also write. Their sweet spot is artists who already have a small catalog of strong demos.

How to get in the door: They host quarterly "open ears" sessions where you can submit 3 songs digitally. Follow their Nashville Instagram account for announcements — submissions typically open for 48 hours and fill up fast.

2. Relative Music Group

A newer boutique publisher that's made waves by signing three writers who landed cuts on major-label country releases last year. They're looking for: lyric-forward writers and topliners who can craft hooks. They favor writers who are also active performers.

How to get in the door: Relative's creative director regularly attends rounds at The Listening Room and Bluebird Cafe. They've signed two writers directly after hearing them perform in-the-round. Get on those stages.

3. Prescription Songs Nashville

The Nashville arm of the Lukasz Gottwald (Dr. Luke) publishing company has been quietly building a country-pop roster. They're looking for: writers who can bridge the gap between mainstream country and pop. Think: melodic sensibility with Nashville storytelling.

How to get in the door: They accept cold submissions via their website, but referrals from existing writers are the fastest path. Attend NSAI events — several Prescription writers are active members.

The common thread: all three want writers who are actively writing and performing, not waiting for permission. Build your catalog, play live, and make yourself visible. The deals follow the work.

NASHVILLE VENUES

The Bluebird Cafe just updated their submission process for new artists — and if you haven't applied recently, the rules have changed.

As of March 2026, the Bluebird no longer accepts walk-up sign-ups for their open mic nights. Instead, all performers must submit through their new online portal, which opens the first Monday of each month. You upload one song (recorded on your phone is fine), a short bio, and your social links. They review submissions within two weeks.

The good news: they've expanded from 8 open mic slots to 12, and they're prioritizing Nashville-based artists who haven't played the room before. If you've been on the waitlist, resubmit through the new system — old submissions didn't carry over.

But the Bluebird isn't the only game in town. Here are 5 other Nashville venues where you can get a real audience and real industry attention:

1. The Listening Room Cafe (SoBro) — Nashville's best-kept secret for serious songwriters. In-the-round format, attentive audiences, and publishers actually show up. Apply through their website; they book 2-3 months out.

2. The 5 Spot (East Nashville) — More eclectic and genre-flexible than the Bluebird. Monday night residencies are a great way to build a local following. They're open to rock, soul, funk, and Americana — not just country.

3. 3rd & Lindsley — A proper listening room with a real sound system. Great for artists ready to step up from coffee shop gigs. They book through a talent buyer, so you'll need a press kit and some live footage.

4. The Basement / The Basement East — Two connected venues: The Basement (intimate, 250 cap) and The Basement East (larger, 800 cap). Industry showcases happen regularly. This is where you play when you're ready to be "discovered."

5. Douglas Corner Cafe — Old-school Nashville songwriter venue with a loyal local crowd. Less industry pressure, more about the craft. Perfect for testing new material in a supportive environment.

The strategy: start with open mics (Bluebird, Listening Room), build to booked shows (5 Spot, Douglas Corner), then graduate to showcase rooms (3rd & Lindsley, Basement East). Each venue serves a different stage of your Nashville journey.

GEAR & STUDIOS

Nashville has more recording studios per capita than any city in America. That's great for options — and overwhelming if you're trying to figure out where to record your next project without blowing your budget.

Here's a practical guide to Nashville's studio landscape, organized by budget:

Under $50/hour — Home Studios & Project Rooms

Nashville is full of talented engineers running professional-quality setups out of converted garages, guest houses, and spare bedrooms. These rooms won't have a Neve console, but many have excellent signal chains and — more importantly — engineers who know how to get great sounds fast.

  • Where to find them: SoundBetter Nashville listings, the Nashville Musicians Facebook group, and NSAI member referrals
  • What to expect: $30-50/hour, usually including the engineer. Bring your own session players or come prepared to track yourself.
  • Best for: Demos, singer-songwriter recordings, podcast production, and rough mixes

$75-150/hour — Mid-Range Professional Studios

This is the sweet spot for indie artists who want real studio quality without major-label pricing. Rooms like The Bomb Shelter, Skinny Elephant, and Welcome to 1979 offer excellent gear, treated rooms, and experienced staff.

  • What you get: A proper control room, quality microphone selection, and usually a tracking room big enough for a full band
  • Best for: Album tracks, singles intended for release, and sessions where you need a drummer in a real room

$200+/hour — The Hit Factories

Studios like Blackbird, Sound Emporium, and RCA Studio A are where the records you grew up on were made. These rooms have irreplaceable acoustics, vintage gear collections, and staff engineers who've worked with everyone.

  • Why pay the premium: The room sound itself becomes part of your record. You can't replicate Blackbird Studio C's drum sound in a home studio.
  • Pro tip: Many of these studios offer "lockout" rates for overnight or weekend sessions that can cut costs by 30-40%. Ask about off-peak pricing.

Regardless of budget, always book a one-hour test session before committing to a full project. The vibe between you and the engineer matters as much as the gear on the wall.

NASHVILLE ARTIST POLL

What's the biggest challenge for artists in Nashville right now?

NASHVILLE COMMUNITY

The Nashville Independent Artist Collective (NIAC) just launched something the city has needed for a long time: a free monthly showcase specifically for unsigned, independent artists. No pay-to-play. No ticket minimums. Just music.

The first showcase was held last month at Marathon Music Works and drew over 200 attendees, including A&R reps from three labels and a handful of publishing scouts. Six artists performed 20-minute sets, selected from open applications.

How to apply: Submit through the NIAC website by the 15th of each month. They're looking for artists with original material and a live-ready set. Genre doesn't matter — last month's lineup included a country singer-songwriter, an R&B duo, a folk artist, and an indie rock band.

But NIAC isn't the only way to connect this month. Here's what else is happening:

Songwriting Circles

  • NSAI Song Critiques — Every Tuesday at the NSAI office on Music Row. Bring a song, get honest feedback from professional writers and publishers. Free for members ($200/year membership is worth it).
  • The Songwriting Underground — A newer group that meets every other Thursday at various East Nashville locations. Less formal than NSAI, more genre-diverse. Find them on Instagram @songwritingunderground.

Open Mics Worth Your Time

  • The Bluebird Cafe — Monday nights (new submission process, see above)
  • The Listening Room — Wednesday open mic, sign-up opens online at noon the day of
  • Bobby's Idle Hour — Thursday nights, casual and welcoming. Great for testing brand-new songs.

Networking Events

  • Music City Creators Meetup — First Wednesday of every month at Pinewood Social. Songwriters, producers, engineers, and managers. No pitch decks, just conversations over coffee.
  • Women in Nashville Music (WIN) — Monthly mixer for women and non-binary folks in the Nashville music industry. Rotating venues. Follow @womeninmusic_nash for dates.

Nashville's greatest asset isn't its studios or its history — it's the fact that the person sitting next to you at any coffee shop might be the co-writer, producer, or manager who changes your career. Show up. That's step one.

🎧 Trivia Answer

A) Ryman Auditorium

The Ryman Auditorium — the "Mother Church of Country Music" — has hosted more Grammy-nominated artists than any other Nashville venue. Originally built as a church in 1892, it served as the home of the Grand Ole Opry from 1943 to 1974 and continues to be one of the most revered performance halls in the world.
