INDUSTRY PULSE

Universal Music Group's Q4 2025 earnings contained a number that should concern everyone in the music business: streaming revenue growth decelerated to 8.2%, down from 14.1% in Q4 2024 and 22.3% in Q4 2023.

On the earnings call, CEO Lucian Grainge framed this as mature market normalization. But dig into the regional breakdown and a more complex picture emerges.

The Good: Latin America (+31%) and Southeast Asia (+44%) continue to explode. Spotify's expansion into Pakistan and Bangladesh is driving subscriber numbers that weren't in anyone's forecast.

The Bad: North America grew just 3.1% — effectively flat when adjusted for inflation. Europe was 5.4%. These are the markets that generate 68% of UMG's streaming revenue.

The Ugly: Average revenue per user (ARPU) dropped 6% globally for the second consecutive quarter, even as subscription prices increased.

What this means for indie artists:

  1. Per-stream rates will continue to compress in mature markets
  2. Growth opportunities are increasingly in non-English-language markets
  3. Diversification beyond streaming (sync, live, merch, direct-to-fan) isn't optional anymore — it's survival

The streaming gold rush isn't over, but the easy gains are.

AI & MUSIC LAB

Both Suno and Udio released major model updates in February 2026. We tested both extensively across 10 genres with identical prompts.

Production Quality: Udio v2 wins Udio's new model produces masters that are genuinely hard to distinguish from human-produced tracks in electronic genres. Suno v4 still has a slight AI sheen — a flatness in the 2-4kHz range that trained ears can detect.

Lyrical Coherence: Suno v4 wins Suno's lyric generation took a massive leap. It can now maintain narrative coherence across verses and even deploy metaphor. Udio's lyrics feel more generic.

Vocal Realism: Draw Both models still struggle with breath placement, uniform vibrato, and blurred consonants. Neither sounds like a real singer to trained ears.

The Legal Landscape: Three major lawsuits filed in Q1 2026 are challenging AI music generators' use of copyrighted training data. If courts rule against them, both may need to retrain on licensed-only datasets.

Our Take: AI music tools are production assistants, not replacements. Use them for demo creation, beat sketching, and ideation. Don't use them for final releases or sync placements where copyright chain-of-title matters.

The gap between AI and human music is closing. But closing and closed are very different things.

MONEY MOVES

Hipgnosis Songs Fund, once the poster child of music catalog investing, is selling $400M worth of song catalogs at roughly 60 cents on the dollar. For an industry that spent the last five years telling everyone that songs are the new real estate, this is a reality check.

Between 2018-2022, Hipgnosis acquired over 65,000 songs — including catalogs from Shakira, Neil Young, and Blondie — at multiples of 18-25x annual net publisher's share (NPS). The thesis: streaming growth would push earnings higher every year.

That thesis assumed 15-20% annual streaming growth forever. It was wrong.

A catalog bought at 20x NPS needs to earn its purchase price back over 20 years BEFORE generating any return. If streaming growth slows, those 20 years become 25 or 30. Add rising interest rates, and the entire model goes underwater.

What's selling and at what price:

  • Pre-2000 rock/pop catalogs: 8-12x NPS (down from 18-22x)
  • 2000-2015 hip-hop catalogs: 10-14x NPS (down from 16-20x)
  • Recent hits (post-2020): Still holding at 14-18x NPS

For artists considering selling their catalogs, the window of peak pricing has closed. For buyers with patient capital, the next 18 months will offer acquisition opportunities not seen since 2017.

GLOBAL MARKETS

Africa's music streaming market grew 42% in 2025, making it the fastest-growing region in the global music industry for the third consecutive year.

The Platform War:

  • Spotify has 18M subscribers across Africa (up from 11M in 2024)
  • Boomplay leads with 95M monthly active users but only 4M paid subscribers
  • YouTube Music is the actual market leader by usage at ~140M regular users
  • Apple Music remains a premium niche with ~3M subscribers

The defining feature of African streaming is the freemium gap. In Nigeria, 87% of streaming is ad-supported versus 32% in the US.

What's driving growth:

  1. Mobile data costs dropped 35% across Sub-Saharan Africa in 2025
  2. Spotify opened offices in Lagos, Nairobi, and Johannesburg with local editorial teams
  3. Afrobeats' global reach is creating a feedback loop: international success drives domestic streaming

Revenue reality:

Despite user growth, Africa generated approximately $450M in recorded music revenue in 2025 — less than 2% of the global total. Per-stream rates average $0.001-0.002, roughly half the global average.

For rights holders, Africa represents the most asymmetric bet in music: massive audience, improving infrastructure, and still-low entry costs. The companies investing now will own the next decade.

FESTIVAL ECONOMY

Thirteen major music festivals have cancelled or taken a year off since January 2025. The list includes Bonnaroo, Primavera Sound LA, Afropunk Brooklyn, and Glastonbury's planned expansion.

The Cost Crisis:

Headliner fees have increased 340% since 2019. Post-pandemic, artists recognized that festivals needed them more than they needed festivals. When Taylor Swift's Eras Tour can gross $2B, why headline a festival for $5M?

Insurance costs are up 60%. Production costs are up 45%. Ticket prices hit a ceiling in 2024 when multiple festivals saw 20-30% drops in advance sales.

What's surviving:

  1. Niche festivals with devoted audiences (Pickathon, Moogfest, Levitation)
  2. City festivals using existing infrastructure (Pitchfork Chicago, Governors Ball)
  3. Luxury festivals serving price-insensitive audiences (Coachella, Fuji Rock)

What's dying:

Mid-market destination festivals with 20-50K capacity and $250-350 ticket prices. This segment requires headliner-driven marketing but can't afford headliner fees.

The New Model:

Expect more festivals to adopt the residency model — multi-weekend events at permanent venues with rotating lineups. Lower costs, recurring revenue, and no weather risk. AEG and Live Nation are already acquiring permanent festival sites.

The festival bubble burst, but live music isn't dying. It's restructuring around economics that actually work.

📈 Trivia Answer

C) 2023

According to IFPI's Global Music Report, worldwide recorded music revenue hit $28.6 billion in 2023 — finally eclipsing the 1999 peak of $23.3B. It took the industry 24 years and the rise of streaming to recover from the digital disruption that began with Napster. Adjusted for inflation, the 1999 peak was ~$41B, so there's still a gap in real terms.
