Understanding Streaming Royalties

A practical guide to how artists get paid from streaming platforms, what the rates really look like, and how to maximize every stream.

Streaming now accounts for over 80% of recorded music revenue worldwide. Yet most independent artists don't fully understand how they get paid or how much money they're leaving on the table. This guide breaks it all down in plain language.

1 How Streaming Royalties Actually Work

Streaming platforms don't pay a fixed amount per stream. Instead, they use a pro-rata model: they pool all subscription and ad revenue for a given period, then divide it among rights holders based on each song's share of total streams.

There are two main types of royalties generated by a single stream:

  • Recording royalties — paid to whoever owns the master recording (you, your label, or your distributor). This is the larger share.
  • Publishing royalties — paid to the songwriter and publisher. These flow through collection societies, mechanical rights organizations, and your PRO.

If you wrote the song and own your masters, you're entitled to both. But you need the right registrations in place to collect them.

3 The Royalty Collection Chain

Money from a single stream can flow through multiple organizations before it reaches you. Here's how to make sure you're collecting everything:

Recording Side

  • Distributor (DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby, AWAL) — delivers your music to platforms and collects recording royalties on your behalf. They take a flat fee or percentage.
  • SoundExchange (US) — collects digital performance royalties from non-interactive streams (Pandora, SiriusXM, internet radio). Register even if you use a distributor.

Publishing Side

  • PRO (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, PRS, SOCAN) — collects public performance royalties when your song is streamed, played on radio, or performed live.
  • Mechanical rights organization (Harry Fox Agency, MLC in the US; MCPS in the UK) — collects mechanical royalties generated by on-demand streams.
  • Publishing administrator (Songtrust, TuneCore Publishing, Sentric) — files registrations worldwide and collects global publishing royalties you'd otherwise miss.
Action step: At minimum, register with your PRO, your country's mechanical rights body, and SoundExchange (if US-based). Consider a publishing administrator if you want global coverage without signing a publishing deal.

4 How to Maximize Your Streaming Revenue

  • Release consistently. Artists who release every 4-6 weeks maintain algorithmic momentum. Singles outperform albums for streaming strategy.
  • Focus on saves and playlist adds, not just plays. Spotify's algorithm weighs saves heavily. Ask fans to save your song, not just listen.
  • Target high-value markets. A stream from Norway or the US pays 3-5x more than a stream from a lower-CPM country. Tailor ads accordingly.
  • Pitch playlists early. Submit to Spotify editorial at least 7 days before release. Use SubmitHub and PlaylistPush for independent curators.
  • Claim all platforms. Ensure your artist profile is claimed on Spotify, Apple, Amazon, YouTube, Tidal, Deezer, and Pandora.
  • Optimize metadata. Correct genre tags, songwriter credits, and ISRC codes ensure royalties are tracked accurately across all platforms.
  • Don't ignore YouTube. Register with a Content ID service to monetize fan-uploaded videos, covers, and user-generated content featuring your music.

5 Common Mistakes That Cost You Money

  • Not registering as a songwriter with your PRO. Your distributor only collects recording royalties. Without a PRO registration, you forfeit performance royalties entirely.
  • Ignoring mechanical royalties. In the US, register with The MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective). This is free and legally required for platforms to pay you.
  • Wrong metadata or missing credits. If your legal name doesn't match your PRO registration, royalties can go uncollected for years. Double-check every release.
  • Using only one distributor and assuming you're covered. Distributors handle the recording side. You still need separate registrations for publishing, performance, and neighboring rights.
  • Buying fake streams. Platforms detect artificial streaming and will remove your music, withhold royalties, and potentially ban your account permanently.
Bottom line: Streaming income is real, but it requires setup. Spend one afternoon registering with every organization listed above, and you'll collect royalties from every angle for every song you ever release.
